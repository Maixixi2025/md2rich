Best AI Text Detectors 2026 for Markdown Writers

In July 2026 the AI-text-detection market has six tools that matter for a Markdown-first writer: Pangram, GPTZero, Originality.ai, Copyleaks, Sapling, and ZeroGPT. They each score the same paragraph differently, each charge a different price, and each flag different tells. This guide walks through the six, lays out the published benchmarks, and shows where md2rich fits so the rewrite that passes the detector is the same text you paste into LinkedIn, X Articles, Substack, or Bluesky without losing the formatting.

The reason this matters in July 2026 specifically: Pangram's July 9 report AI Content Is Everywhere on Social Media, Especially LinkedIn found that more than 40% of LinkedIn longform posts (over 250 words) flag as fully AI-generated. LinkedIn accounts for two-thirds of every post the Pangram Chrome extension flagged across the five platforms it tracked. If you draft in Markdown and paste into LinkedIn, your reader is now running an extension-level detector over your post before they finish reading the first paragraph. The detector is part of the editor's workflow, and your Markdown draft has to survive it.

The other twenty-plus are either wrappers around the same transformer or hosted versions of an academic detector (Binoculars, GLTR, FastDetectGPT) that has not been retrained on GPT-5-class output.

Pangram — the social-platform detector that started the 2026 conversation

Pangram is the only one of the six that has published a peer-reviewed detector (the EditLens ICLR 2026 paper) and then shipped Open Pangram, an open-weights release that lets researchers reproduce the benchmarks. That matters because every other vendor's "99% accuracy" claim is unverifiable. Pangram's claims are.

Pangram is also the only detector that ships a social-platform feed scanner as a first-class feature: the Professional tier at $65/mo auto-scans your Substack, LinkedIn, and X feeds so you can see which of your own posts read as AI. That is the feature that produced the July 9 LinkedIn dataset. If you publish on LinkedIn, Pangram is the only detector whose product roadmap matches the platform you actually post on.

Pricing as of July 13, 2026: free 4 scans/day; Individual $20/mo for 600 credits; Professional $65/mo for 3,000 credits plus the feed scanner; Developer API at $25 per 500 credits (1 credit = 1,000 words); Team $20/seat/mo (minimum 2 seats). Enterprise and Education tiers are quoted.

What Pangram catches that the others miss

  • Pangram's claim is the highest accuracy on paraphrased AI text, the case where humans rewrite an LLM draft in their own voice and then run it through a detector. GPTZero and ZeroGPT both drop to under 50% on paraphrased AI; Pangram stays above 90%.
  • Pangram has a dedicated AI-assistance classifier separate from the fully-AI classifier. If you use Copilot to suggest a sentence and then write the rest yourself, Pangram labels the draft "AI-assisted" instead of forcing a fully-human / fully-AI binary.
  • Pangram can detect GPT-5.6 and Claude Sonnet 5 outputs within 24 hours of release. Other vendors typically need two to four weeks of customer-reported false positives before they ship a model update.

Originality.ai — the suite for agencies and publishers

Originality.ai sits in a different position: it is a content-integrity suite, not just a detector. The Pro tier at $12.95/mo (annual billing, $14.95/mo monthly) bundles AI detection, plagiarism checking, readability scoring, fact-checking, and grammar in one dashboard, with 2,000 credits per month (1 credit = 100 words). The Enterprise tier at $136.58/mo (annual) raises credits to 15,000 and adds team seats at $8.62/seat/mo.

Originality's strongest claim is on internal benchmarks: their published table shows Originality at 97.09% on GPT-4 human-vs-AI vs GPTZero at 63.77% and Sapling at 66.66%. Those numbers are internal, but they have been reproduced in third-party academic studies at lower-but-still-leading rates. If you want one dashboard that flags AI, scans for plagiarism, and tells you whether your Flesch-Kincaid is on target for a Substack audience, Originality is the right pick.

Where Originality falls short

  • The free tier is a teaser; you can paste a paragraph, but you cannot save a scan or run plagiarism on it.
  • Subscription credits expire monthly. If you publish seasonally, you lose what you paid for.
  • Originality does not have a feed scanner, so it cannot tell you which of your existing LinkedIn posts read as AI. You would have to paste each one in manually.

GPTZero — the academic-vertical incumbent

GPTZero was the first mainstream detector to hit scale in 2023 and is still the one most teachers and HR teams know by name. The company reports 17 million users and 1 million educators on the homepage. The free tier lets you paste up to 10,000 characters per scan, which is roughly 2,000 words — enough for a long LinkedIn post but not enough for a full Substack essay.

GPTZero's product is wider than detection: hallucination detection, plagiarism checking, grammar, authorship verification, and an AI Reviewer that grades essays. If you are buying for a school or a recruiting team, GPTZero is the right call. If you are an individual writer publishing on your own platform, you are paying for features you will never use.

The honest caveat

GPTZero's own accuracy on independent benchmarks trails Pangram and Originality by 20-30 percentage points, especially on paraphrased AI and on mixed human-AI drafts. The free tier is generous; the paid tier is priced for institutions, not for solo writers. If you are a writer, treat GPTZero as a sanity check, not as your primary detector.

Copyleaks — the multilingual and enterprise pick

Copyleaks is the only one of the six that markets AI detection across 30+ languages and across video and image formats in addition to text. If you publish in Spanish, Japanese, French, German, or Chinese in addition to English, Copyleaks is the only detector on this list whose benchmarks cover your second language.

The product is enterprise-priced: there is no published per-seat monthly price on the pricing page; you have to "Get a Demo" and talk to sales. That makes Copyleaks a poor fit for a solo writer but a strong fit for a publisher with a translation team or a university with non-English thesis submissions.

Copyleaks also runs the only AI image and AI video detectors with the same accuracy as the text detector. If you cross-post LinkedIn carousels or YouTube Shorts and you need to verify that the assets are original, Copyleaks is the only one-stop shop.

Sapling — the generous free tier for long drafts

Sapling's AI detector is the most generous free detector on this list for length: 2,000 characters per query for free users, 100,000 characters per query on Pro. That covers roughly 20,000 words — a full Substack essay or a Medium draft — in a single scan. Sapling also publishes per-sentence perplexity scores so you can see exactly which sentences the detector thinks are AI.

The detector works by combining perplexity and burstiness scores; the per-sentence view is the part that matters most for a writer. If you draft a 1,500-word Markdown post and paste it into Sapling, you can scan the page, find the three sentences that light up as low-perplexity (the AI tells), and rewrite only those three sentences instead of the whole draft. That surgical workflow is faster than running the whole draft through Pangram.

Sapling's caveats

  • Sapling does not have a social-platform feed scanner.
  • Sapling's multilingual coverage is limited; English benchmarks are strong, Spanish and German are spotty.
  • Sapling's accuracy claim of 97% with less than 3% false-positive rate is on their own benchmarks; third-party tests place it in the 80-90% range.

ZeroGPT — the bundled suite with a conflict of interest

ZeroGPT markets itself as the most accurate detector but bundles the AI Humanizer and Paraphraser inside the same product. That is a structural conflict of interest: the same company sells you an AI detector and a tool to bypass AI detection. Take any claim from a vendor that sells both halves of the same market with a grain of salt.

The free tier lets you paste up to 15,000 characters per scan, which is generous. The paid tier is among the cheapest, around $9.99/mo for the basic plan, and adds API access, batch uploads, and PDF report exports. If you need a cheap detector for spot checks on short excerpts and you do not care about the conflict of interest, ZeroGPT is fine. For a workflow you actually trust, pick one of the other five.

How detectors actually decide: the four tells

Every detector in the table above runs some variant of the same four signals. Knowing them is the difference between rewriting a paragraph and rewriting a draft.

1. Perplexity (how predictable each word is)

A high-perplexity sentence surprises you. A low-perplexity sentence reads as the obvious next word. AI text trends low-perplexity because the model is sampling the most likely token at each step. The classic AI tell is a sentence where every word is the one you would have guessed: "This approach provides significant value for teams looking to streamline their workflow." No human writes that sentence on a first draft. Rewrite with one specific noun, one number, or one verb that the model would not have picked: "Our 7-person editorial team cut draft review time from 3 days to 6 hours."

2. Burstiness (how varied paragraph length is)

Human writing alternates long and short paragraphs. AI writing tends toward uniform length — every paragraph is 4-6 sentences, every sentence is 15-25 words. After you draft your Markdown post, scan it: if every paragraph is the same length, vary two of them. The detector's burstiness signal drops immediately.

3. Lexicon (the AI-vocabulary list)

GPTZero publishes an "AI Vocabulary" feature that lists the words most over-represented in AI text. The recurring list is consistent across detectors: delve, leverage, robust, comprehensive, streamline, navigate (as a verb), landscape (as a metaphor), tapestry, in today's [noun], it's worth noting, it is important to note. After you draft, run Ctrl+F on this list. If any of these phrases appears in your draft, replace with a verb that names the action: "We surveyed 40 teams" instead of "We delve into how teams leverage robust workflows."

4. Structure (the parallel three-list tell)

AI text loves parallel three-item lists with the same grammatical shape: fast, reliable, and scalable. Human writing breaks the shape at least once per post. The fix is mechanical: when you write a three-item list, change one item to a fragment, a number, or a question.

The Markdown-first detection-safe workflow

For a Markdown-first writer in 2026 the workflow is five steps. All of them run in your browser or in plain text — no upload to a server, no AI model in the loop on your draft.

  1. Draft in Markdown. Plain text in Obsidian, iA Writer, VS Code, or a .md file. Use your own voice. Do not paste AI-generated text into the draft unless you plan to rewrite every paragraph by hand.
  2. Convert to rich text in your browser. Open md2rich, paste the Markdown into the editor, copy the rich text output. The conversion runs entirely in your browser — your draft never touches a server, which keeps the draft out of any training pipeline and out of any detector's reference corpus.
  3. Paste into LinkedIn, X Articles, Substack, or Bluesky long-form. Headings, lists, links, and tables all land intact because md2rich produces Word-compatible HTML, not Markdown-flavored HTML.
  4. Run the published post through Pangram. If you are on the free tier (4 scans/day), run only the posts you are most worried about. If you are on Individual or Professional, run every post.
  5. Rewrite only the flagged sentences. Use the per-sentence perplexity view in Sapling if you want to skip straight to the bad sentences, or rewrite by hand based on the four tells above. Paste back into the platform, republish if needed.

The principle is the same in every case: write in Markdown, convert to rich text in the browser, paste once, then audit. The Markdown stays as your canonical source so you never lose formatting if you need to republish.

FAQ

Which AI detector is most accurate in 2026?

Pangram and Originality.ai both report peer-reviewed benchmarks in the 95-99% range on GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 5, and Gemini 3 outputs. Pangram's open-weights release in March 2026 (Open Pangram) lets researchers reproduce its numbers, which is the only benchmark claim that holds up under scrutiny. For a Markdown-first writer, start with Pangram's free 4 scans/day, then add Originality.ai's $12.95/mo Pro tier when you scale past that.

Will AI detectors flag my Markdown draft as AI?

A clean Markdown draft with your own sentence patterns, varied paragraph lengths, and concrete details (names, dates, numbers) almost always reads as human. The flags usually come from default LLM tells: em-dash overuse, em-dash + colon opener, parallel three-item lists, hedging adverbs like significantly or robust, and zero first-person references. Run your draft through Pangram before you paste; if any sentence scores over 70% AI, rewrite that sentence with one concrete detail only you would know.

Do I need a paid detector or is the free tier enough?

For a single writer publishing one to three LinkedIn longform posts a week, Pangram's free 4 scans/day or Sapling's 2,000-character free window is enough. Sapling is the most generous free detector for long drafts (100,000 characters on Pro). When you scale to daily publishing or want plagiarism + AI in one place, Originality.ai at $12.95/mo Pro or Pangram at $20/mo Individual becomes the right tier. ZeroGPT's free tier is fine for spot checks on short excerpts but its free daily allowance is the smallest of the six.

Does paraphrasing or humanizing bypass AI detectors?

It depends which tool. ZeroGPT and Sapling both sell AI Humanizer or paraphrasing features inside the same product, which is a clear conflict of interest: the same company writes the AI text and sells you the detector. Independent third-party testing shows these humanizers drop Pangram's detection rate from 95% to around 60%, but only on the first pass; running the output through a second detector still flags it. The reliable de-AI workflow is rewriting in your own voice, not paraphrasing AI text into a slightly different AI text. Draft in Markdown, convert to rich text with md2rich, paste into the platform, then run Pangram once.

Can I detect AI text in languages other than English?

Copyleaks supports 30+ languages and Pangram supports 20+. GPTZero and ZeroGPT claim multilingual coverage but their published benchmarks are English-only. Sapling's detector works on English with limited validation on Spanish, French, and German. For a non-English Markdown draft, run it through Copyleaks first; if you publish in two or more languages, the Pangram Professional tier ($65/mo, 3,000 credits) is the cheapest path to multilingual scans with plagiarism included.
